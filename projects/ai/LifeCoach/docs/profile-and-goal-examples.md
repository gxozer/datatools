# Profile & Goal Examples

**Epic:** [PR-133](https://mgozer.atlassian.net/browse/PR-133)
**Version:** 3.0
**Date:** 2026-06-05

These examples illustrate the breadth of users and goals the Life Coach App is designed to serve. They guide product design, onboarding user experience (UX), artificial intelligence (AI) prompt engineering, tone calibration, and marketing personas.

---

## Core Product Philosophy

### Zero Setup. Maximum Discovery.

The app should require as little from the user as possible. Users do not need to import lists, configure databases, or tell the app what exists in the world. **The app already knows.**

The platform maintains a curated content database covering:
- Pubs, bars, restaurants, and food markets (by city, region, and type)
- Walking and hiking trails
- National parks, nature reserves, and scenic routes
- Museums, galleries, theatres, and cultural venues
- Historical sites and heritage trails
- Sports grounds and stadiums
- Music venues, record fairs, and events
- Michelin-starred and notable restaurants
- Street art and public installations
- Community events, classes, and meetups

When the app detects a user is near — or travelling through — a location with relevant content, it surfaces it **without the user having asked**. The user's only job is to say yes or no.

### One Tap. Invite Sent.

When a user accepts a recommendation, the app sends a calendar invite to the user's email — with the venue name, location, opening hours, and any relevant notes. The user accepts the invite in their calendar app (Google Calendar, Outlook, Apple Calendar). No copy-pasting, no manual entries. The app never writes directly to the user's calendar; it only sends invites that the user chooses to accept or decline.

### The Feedback Loop

After a calendar event time passes, the app sends a short email:
> *"Did you make it to [The Phibsborough House]? A quick review helps others — and builds your reputation on the app."*

The email contains a 5-star rating and a single optional text field. Response is one tap. High-quality text responses contribute to the user's reputation score.

### Reputation

Users build a public reputation score based on:
- Number of reviews submitted
- Quality score (length, detail, helpfulness ratings from other users)
- Consistency (regular reviewers score higher than occasional ones)
- Accuracy (flagged corrections vs. confirmed accuracy of details)

Higher reputation unlocks: priority access to new content, early access to features, reduced subscription cost tiers, and visibility as a trusted reviewer within the community.

### User-Generated Content (UGC)

Users can create their own content:
- **Trails** — a named, ordered sequence of locations (e.g. "Maria's Top 10 Hidden Pubs of Stoneybatter")
- **Lists** — unordered collections (e.g. "Best pintxos in San Sebastián by category")
- **Reviews** — single venue write-ups
- **Route guides** — turn-by-turn walking or driving routes with commentary

Content is published to the platform. Other users can follow, save, and use it. The platform tracks usage metrics per creator.

### Creator Benefits

As a creator's content grows in popularity:

| Milestone | Benefit |
|---|---|
| 100 saves | "Rising Creator" badge; content featured in local search results |
| 500 saves | 1 month Pro free; invitation to early-access beta features |
| 1,000 saves | "Trusted Creator" badge; content promoted in onboarding for new users in that city |
| 5,000 saves | Revenue share on Pro subscriptions driven by creator content (tracked via attribution) |
| 10,000 saves | "Expert" status; featured creator profile; priority support |

Content creators also benefit from reputation multipliers — a highly-rated trail contributes more to your reputation than an unrated one.

## How to Read These Examples

Each profile includes:
- **Who the user is** — background, personality, life context
- **Their goals** — primary and secondary
- **Onboarding** — how the app sets up with minimal user input
- **A typical week** — how the app integrates into daily life
- **Proactive discovery scenarios** — the app surfacing content the user never asked for
- **Calendar and feedback loop** — accept → calendar invite sent → user accepts invite → post-visit email → review → reputation
- **UGC scenarios** — where relevant, how the user creates and benefits from content
- **Setbacks** — missed goals, bad days, life getting in the way
- **Progression** — how the relationship evolves over time

### 1. The Dublin Pub Crawler
> **Maria, 34 — Dublin, Ireland**
> Marketing manager at a tech company. Works from home 4 days a week. Social butterfly — she knows everyone in her neighbourhood. Grew up in Galway, moved to Dublin at 22. Loves local history, storytelling, and the craic. Has strong opinions about what makes a good pub (the answer is never the décor).

**Goals**
- Primary: Visit all the pubs in Dublin
- Secondary: Walk 8,000 steps a day
- Bonus: Document her favourites for others to discover

**Onboarding**
Maria says: *"I want to visit all the pubs in Dublin."* That's it. The app already has a curated, verified database of 847 Dublin pubs with addresses, opening hours, character tags (traditional, sports, music, Victorian, snug), and community reviews. It builds her Dublin map immediately. She confirms she hasn't tracked visits before — starting at 0. The app shows her the 12 nearest pubs and asks if she wants to mark any as already visited. She taps 6. Progress: 6 / 847.

**Typical Week**

*Monday:* Maria is working from home. She has a 75-min lunch break. App detects her location: Phibsborough.
> "The Phibsborough House is 8 mins from you and on your pub list. Quiet Monday crowd. Want to add it to your lunch?"

She taps **Send invite**. The invite arrives in her calendar. A lunch event is created: *"The Phibsborough House — pub visit"*, 1pm–2pm, with address, phone number, and a note: *"Traditional. Known for the snug. On Maria's Dublin list."*

She goes. Has a toasted sandwich and a half-pint. After 2pm she receives an email:
> *"Did you make it to The Phibsborough House? A quick review helps others find it."*

She rates it 5 stars and writes: *"Dark wood, old hurling jerseys, barman called Liam who's been there 22 years. Proper."* Progress: 7 / 847. Reputation +12.

*Wednesday:* App notices she has a 3pm meeting in the city centre and will pass through Stoneybatter on the way.
> "You'll pass Walsh's on your way to your 3pm — it's on your list and 2 mins off your route. Quick drop-in?"

She taps **Send invite**. The invite for *"Walsh's — quick stop"*, 2:40pm–2:55pm, arrives in her calendar. She accepts it. She does it. Post-visit email that evening — she rates it 4 stars. Progress: 8 / 847.

*Friday evening:* App detects that two of Maria's contacts (Aoife and Dave) are also app users nearby and free.
> "Ranelagh has 4 pubs on your list within a 15-min walk. Aoife and Dave are free tonight — want me to suggest the route to them?"

She confirms. App sends Aoife and Dave a shared route. They do 3 of the 4. Three post-visit emails go out. All three leave reviews. Maria's reputation increases for surfacing the route to others.

**Proactive Discovery — Kilkenny Trip**

Maria travels to Kilkenny for a work event. She has never set up a Kilkenny pub goal. The app detects she's in Kilkenny and surfaces content from its database:
> "You're in Kilkenny — one of Ireland's great pub cities. The medieval quarter has 6 pubs within a 10-min walk, including Kyteler's Inn (1324 AD, oldest pub in Ireland) and Tynan's Bridge House. Want to see the map?"

Maria didn't know about Kyteler's Inn. She taps yes. The app adds 2 pubs to her calendar for the afternoon gap she has before dinner. She reviews both. One becomes her favourite pub in Ireland. She writes a long review.

Later that week she creates a trail: *"Kilkenny's 5 Greatest Medieval Pubs"* — her first piece of UGC. Within a month it gets 340 saves. Rising Creator badge unlocked.

**UGC Creator Journey**

Maria has now visited 180 pubs and written detailed reviews for 90 of them. She creates three trails:
- "Kilkenny Medieval Pubs" — 340 saves
- "Dublin's Best Snugs" — 1,100 saves
- "Sunday Afternoon Pubs: No Screens, No Sports TV" — 2,800 saves

The last one goes viral. She is promoted as a featured creator in the onboarding flow for new users in Dublin. Her Pro subscription is credited one month free. She emails her mother: *"I think I'm famous on a pub app."*

Revenue share threshold approaching at 5,000 saves combined.

**Setbacks**

Maria gets a cold in February. App says nothing about pubs for a week. First day back:
> "Good to have you back. 839 to go. No rush."

### 5. The Reluctant Beginner
> **Karen, 52 — Toronto, Canada**
> Office manager. Hasn't exercised in years. Scared of failing again. General Practitioner (GP) mentioned her blood pressure.

**Onboarding**
Karen says: *"I want to get moving but I don't know where to start."* App assigns "Health Starter" profile and begins without asking for weight, measurements, or fitness history. First question: *"What does a good day feel like for you?"* She types: *"Getting outside at least once."* The app builds everything around that.

**Proactive Discovery**

Karen has never configured a walking route. On a Tuesday with a gap in her calendar:
> "You've got 20 mins before your 2pm. There's a flat loop through the park at the end of your street — about 1,200 steps, no hills. Want to add it to your calendar?"

She taps **Send invite**. An invite for *"Short walk — Willowdale Park loop"*, 1:35pm, arrives. She accepts it. She goes.

Post-walk email:
> *"Did you get out for the walk? Even a one-word reply helps — it tells me whether to suggest this again."*

She replies: *"Yes, it was nice actually."* The app notes positive response. Reputation +5.

**Feedback Loop Shaping Recommendations**

Over 3 weeks, Karen's email responses teach the app what she likes:
- Flat routes: always yes
- Routes past a coffee shop: yes
- Early morning suggestions: no response (ignores them)
- Lunchtime suggestions: consistently yes

The app quietly adjusts. It stops sending morning suggestions. It starts noting nearby coffee shops on walking routes.

### 30. The Widower Who Isn't Looking
> **Michael, 60 — Melbourne, Australia** *(continued from Profile 16)*

Michael has been using the app for eight months. He has never enabled dating matching and the app has never suggested he do so. He goes to the walking group every Thursday. He has two people there he'd call friends.

One evening he is looking at the app's settings — he's trying to find where to add another walking route — and he sees the dating matching toggle. He reads the description. He closes the app.

Three weeks later he opens settings again.

He turns it on.

**What the App Does**

Nothing for six weeks. Michael's filters are very specific: 55–65, within 15km. The app surfaces one match. He reads the notification. He closes it without engaging. It expires in seven days.

Two months later, a second match.

This one he opens. The questions the match has set:

*"What do you do on a Sunday morning?"*
*"What's something you've started doing recently that you didn't expect to enjoy?"*

He knows what to write. He answers both. He doesn't hurry.

He sets his own questions: *"What does a good week feel like for you?"* and *"What's a place in Melbourne you think everyone should see?"*

She answers in two days. Her answer to the second question is about the rose garden in the Royal Botanic Gardens.

He reads it three times.

**Design Notes**
- The app never nudged Michael toward dating — he found it himself, on his own timeline
- Filters were respected precisely — the app surfaced very few matches rather than widening criteria to generate activity
- The Q&A gave Michael space to engage slowly and carefully — appropriate for where he is in his life
- The rose garden connection emerged from her genuine answer, not from the app manufacturing a link
- This scenario is included to establish: the dating feature must never feel urgent, pushy, or like it has an agenda for the user

## Summary: Design Principles

1. **The app does the work.** Users say what they want — or don't say anything at all if they're passing somewhere interesting. The app has the content, finds the moment, and asks permission. Users tap yes or no.

2. **Accept = calendar invite.** Every accepted recommendation sends a calendar invite to the user's email. The user accepts it in their own calendar app. The app never writes directly to the calendar — the user remains in control. The accepted invite is the commitment layer.

3. **The feedback loop is email, not in-app.** Post-visit emails are short, easy, and arrive at the right moment. One-word replies are enough. The system learns from every response.

4. **Tone is everything.** Blunt for Laura. Warm for Michael. Dry for Claire. Clinical for Aisha. Gentle for Bill. The same engine speaks in a dozen voices.

5. **Reputation is built quietly.** Users don't have to think about reputation. Reviews accumulate naturally. Benefits arrive when thresholds are crossed — they feel like rewards, not mechanics.

6. **UGC is the moat.** Local knowledge, lived experience, and personal voice cannot be replicated by algorithmic content. Maria's pub trails, Nina's Helsinki swimming guide, and Carlos's pintxos rankings are more useful than any professional database — and the platform owns them.

7. **Creator benefits are real.** Saves, helpfulness votes, and traffic attribution must drive tangible rewards: free subscription time, featured placement, revenue share. Creators must feel the platform values what they make.

8. **Setbacks are not failures.** Missed days, bad weather, illness, grief — the app doesn't nag. It waits, returns gently, and picks up without judgment.

9. **The best moments are small.** Bill talking to the museum volunteer. Michael at the rose garden. Desmond's first roast chicken. The app creates conditions for these moments. It doesn't manufacture them.

10. **The platform learns.** Every email reply, every tap, every no is data. The app's value compounds the longer a user stays.
